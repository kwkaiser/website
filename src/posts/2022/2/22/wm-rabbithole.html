Falling down the window manager rabbithole

The beginning

Desktop linux is, for all its quirks, my most comfortable development environment. Everything is configurable to my exact preference, even when those preferences lead to instability or even unusability, as was often the case when I was familiarizing myself with the operating system & its associated ecosystem.

I think my "nuke and pave" linux phase, where I was learning, breaking, and constantly reinstalling, was in no small part influential on the types of tools I liked to use. Tools that were:

  • Light
  • Easy to install
  • Configurable via plaintext dotfiles

Plaintext configurations deserve their own recognition, but really I'm just fond of any tool that lets me relegate its configuration to an independent, homogeneous, version trackable file I can shove into a git repo with all the other configs.

So right off the bat, that renders the two largest linux desktop environment projects, GNOME and KDE plasma largely off the table. The same is true for a lot of the smaller desktop environments as well.

That leaves window managers, the desktop environment's scrappier and less opinionated relative. Generally without a default application suite and only occasionally composited, they offer a much more skeletal default experience that requires a certain degree of wrenching on to make enjoyable, but ultimately provides more flexibility to focus on the parts of your desktop experience you feel matter. There are a lot of window managers to choose from, but a loose categorization can be built based on some preferences you might have if you're the type of person to stray from the beaten path and install one of these things.

If you like tiling WMs or have an affinity for vim keybindings, you may find yourself at home amongst the tiling WMs like awesome, i3wm, or dwm. If you like the stackable window managers (like those found when using Windows or OSX), you might like openbox.

The other main difference between them that you might care about has to do with flexibility. On a sliding scale of "configured via editing source code" like the suckless dwm and "fully packaged solution" like a desktop environment, I found myself somewhere in the middle.

I want a default configuration, I'm not invested in compiling it, and I'd like my configurations to be independent, but I also want to choose all of my own software. So, ultimately I settled on i3wm. Well, technically swaywm after migrating to wayland.

The itch

So eventually you've got your window manager setup with some keybindings you like and managed to get the holy trinity of:

  • A launcher / window switcher
  • A status bar of some sort
  • Something to set backgrounds

Maybe you throw in a compositor with some cool wobbly window effects & some neat GTK theme you found on the arch wiki. You go to share your setup somewhere like then /r/unixporn subreddit (SFW) only to realize that you've barely scratched the surface when you see the absolutely crazy configurations there. Tree graphs generated from running linux processes, backgrounds that change with the sunset or what's trending on twitter, color pallettes and themes generated from images, and for some reason anime catgirls galore.

The next step the rabbithole is to constantly tweak your configuration, see something cool that someone else did, and get the urge to eventually integrate it into your own setup. Maybe it's having smart LEDs scripted to change their color to whatever RGB value is most expressed on your screen, or replacing all your configuration files with symlinks, or something else entirely. My personal favorite way to take advantage of the plaintext configurations of everything was via pywal, to match my system theme to my background. However, I found a lot of the pallettes to be lacking enough contrast to code with properly (perhaps not unrelated; I first learned I needed glasses around then), so I moved to using pre-defined color pallettes.

Something breaks

Eventually, you've created an absolute mess of tools tied together with baling wire, duct tape, and your constant attention. After a package update, or when you get a bit too ambitious with your xorg configuration or nvidia drivers, the house of cards inevitably comes crashing down.

This isn't all bad though, as long as you can stretch your baling wire to last until your end of term project is finished, because half the fun is tweaking the setup in the first place. Eventually, your new focus is making the new environment easily deployable & configurable with less hassle.

End of the rabbithole

For many, I think it's possible to sink endless quantities of time into this sort of project, until you have animations playing during your bootloader and fibonacci-sequenced window division with your tiling WM, but I eventually settled on a setup focused on deployability and configuring just three things day to day:

  • Backgrounds
  • Fonts
  • Color pallettes

It's pretty much all accomplished through copying templated configution files & substituting in hex color values with sed bash scripts. If this is the sort of thing you're interested in, the code can be found here. This setup has already proven pretty handy after breaking my setup, which happens less often nowadays, but I suppose old habits die hard.
