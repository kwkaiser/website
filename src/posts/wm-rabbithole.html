Falling down the window manager rabbithole

Desktop linux is, for all its quirks, my most comfortable development environment; really for just two primary reasons:

  • I like writing code in an environment similar to where it will execute, which makes linux easy pickings for someone doing backend web dev work
  • Your capacity to customize your development environment is a function of how masochistic you are about your configurations

The cool thing about linux desktop environments & window managers is that if you've ever had a weird idea for how you wish you could interact with your computer, chance is someone gave a shot at manifesting that idea & it has a zealous following of people who swear by it as a productivity booster.

Want an "out of the box" experience? One of the big desktop environments, like GNOME or KDE Plasma are probably right for you. Love vim keybindings and hate your mouse? A tiling WM like i3wm might be more your speed. If you're allergic to desktop icons and want to navigate through nested tree menus to launch everything, a stacking WM like openbox would suit your fancy.

With respect to window management & presentation, the general pattern I've noticed is that the further you trend from a traditional, mouse driven, floating window focused GUI, the smaller projects become & the more configuration responsibilities are levied on the user. Even after you've settled on how you want your environment to look, you have to choose from options that have their own spin on things you wouldn't have expected to care about before; like whether a compositor is built in, or how it's compiled, or how well it supports multiple monitors. All these factors end up leading to a lot of options.

So, eventually you just pick one. One a sliding scale of "full package," like a desktop environment, and "only configurable through editing source code," like suckless software, I found myself close to the middle and ended up settling on i3wm.
