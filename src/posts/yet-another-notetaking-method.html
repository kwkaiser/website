Yet Another Notetaking Method

2022-03-23

Or, how I learned to stop worrying and love the text files

Defining notes

In my mind, notes are kind of a weird thing. I think there's a tendency to forget that you're writing notes for yourself, and you're allowed (perhaps even encouraged) to take shortcuts best facilitated by whatever quirks of consciousness are unique to you. With respect to the balancing act of trying to capture enough detail in your notes, but not too much, I've always tended towards the latter rather than the former. With that in mind, I think it's helpful to make the distinction between notes and documentation. I would argue that "notes" are simple, make sense to those who wrote them, and capture adequate detail. Alternatively, I'd suggest that "documentation" is meant to capture as much detail as possible, but remain readable to a broad or target audience.

With that definition, I wanted to make a notetaking system that was:

  • Simple
  • As immune to lock-in or deprecating tech as possible
  • Ergonomic
  • Distributed
  • Encrypted
  • Scriptable

Existing solutions

Perhaps unsurprisingly, there are a lot of existing solutions out there. There're of course a bunch of proprietary or corporately sponsored solutions, like Apple notes, or Microsoft OneNote, etc. I ruled these out because they were so closely tied with the company that made them. Other solutions, like obsidian, notion, or nextcloud notes either felt like they came with too much baggage/dependencies, required subscriptions to have sync, were tailored towards teams (e.g. were addressing "documentation"), or were just not that comfy to use.

anchor

Well, what I eventually decide on? GPG encrypted plain text files in a directory tree. I know, I know. It seems like a pretty regressive solution, but think about it: at the end of the day, there's a ton of tooling in the Linux ecosystem made to work with text files. I get to use an editor of my choice. Really, it all boils down to just a few items:

  • password store does most of the heavy lifting, frankly. It's described as a password manager but I think it's a pretty ergonomic way to manage any encrypted text file. It smooths some of the rougher edges of working with GPG & auto-committing new files to a git repo. All the output is scriptable, but a lot of the functionality you might want to built in a script (like grepping through the encrypted files) may already be built in.
  • fzf makes searching through all the notes dirt simple for quick referencing.

All the ergonomics, you build anything you want for yourself. Really I only have two scripts; one for creating a datetime stamped file for journaling, and another for creating named notes for ideas, todo lists, recipes, places I've eaten that were worth remembering, etc. Everything is easily syncable between devices, as long as the git repo is accessible through ssh or something else on one of your servers.

Flaws

As much as I like this system, it does have some flaws. It's not particularly well suited for phone note taking; merely phone note referencing. All the scripted solutions you may have built for yourself on your desktop or laptop may not carry over as well to your phone.
